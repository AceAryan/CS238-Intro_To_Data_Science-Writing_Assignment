2 — Global Trajectory

The Pandemic Accelerates

Global weekly new cases rose monotonically from March through July 2020, with no sign of a plateau by the data cutoff. Europe saw an early peak and partial containment by May; the Americas, South-East Asia, and Africa were still in steep ascent.

Global weekly new cases & deaths (Jan–Jul 2020) — from day_wise.csv
Weekly new cases rose from near-zero in January to 1.75 million in the last full week of July 2020 — a 3,150× increase in 6 months. Deaths peaked around April 12 (47,170/week) then declined as case fatality rates fell, before rising again in July as the Americas surged.
Weekly new cases — top 5 countries (from full_grouped.csv)
USA Brazil India Russia UK
Russia's flattening is striking: after a rapid rise through April, weekly new cases plateaued around 70–75K and then declined — consistent with early lockdown effectiveness. The UK also showed a sharp first-wave peak and near-suppression by July. In stark contrast, the USA, Brazil, and India were all still climbing steeply at data cutoff.
Median 1-week % increase in confirmed cases — by WHO Region (country_wise_latest.csv)
Europe had the lowest median growth rate (4.3%/week), reflecting partial post-first-wave stabilisation. Africa (10.6%), Americas (13.0%), and South-East Asia (7.1%) were all still in active community spread phases.
3 — Hypotheses

Four Testable Claims

H1 — Confirmed ✓
More testing reveals more cases: test capacity is a primary driver of detected case rates
Pearson r on log-transformed Tests/1M vs Cases/1M: r = 0.621p < 0.00001 (n = 173). Countries testing more per capita consistently report higher detection rates — not because they are more infected, but because they find what is already there. This has major implications for CFR: low-testing countries show artificially elevated CFRs.
H2 — Confirmed ✓
Europe suffered significantly higher mortality per capita than Asia
Mann-Whitney U test: U = 1547p < 0.00001. Europe median: 100 deaths/M, Asia median: 19 deaths/M — a 5.3× difference. Europe's early under-preparedness, older demographics, and care-home transmission drove far worse outcomes in this first wave.
H3 — Confirmed ✓
Americas were still accelerating while Europe was stabilising by July 2020
Mann-Whitney U on 1-week growth rates: U = 1354p = 0.0012. Americas median 13.0%/week vs Europe 4.3%/week. Of the top 10 Americas countries, 7 had weekly growth rates above 10%, with Colombia (26%) and Argentina (28%) surging.
H4 — Not Supported ~
South America had the highest CFR of any continent
Mann-Whitney U: U = 1183p = 0.39 — not significant. South America median CFR (2.81%) is higher than the global median (2.21%), but Europe actually dominates the worst-CFR list: UK (15.2%), Belgium (14.8%), Italy (14.3%), France (13.7%). SA's high case counts with moderate CFRs reflect different testing and demographic profiles.
4 — H1 Analysis: The Testing Paradox

Testing Is Destiny

The single most important confound in COVID-19 data is testing capacity. The scatter below (worldometer_data.csv, n = 173) shows that Tests/1M population explains a substantial portion of variance in detected Cases/1M — on a log scale, r = 0.621.

5 — H2 Analysis: Continental Mortality

Europe and the Americas Pay a Higher Price

Deaths per million population is the fairest cross-country comparator, correcting for population size. South America (median 157/M) and Europe (100/M) far exceed Africa (7/M) and Asia (19/M). The Mann-Whitney test confirms the Europe vs Asia difference is not random.

6 — CFR by WHO Region

Europe's CFR Is an Outlier — But So Is Its Context

Aggregate CFR across WHO regions (deaths ÷ confirmed cases) reveals a 3.8× range from 1.7% (Africa) to 6.4% (Europe). However, these numbers must be read alongside testing rates: Africa's low CFR is partly a testing artefact, while Europe's high CFR reflects both genuine severity and low early testing.

WHO Region: CFR % vs median weekly growth rate (country_wise_latest.csv)
The inverse relationship between CFR and growth rate by region tells a coherent story: Europe had the highest CFR (first-wave severity) but the lowest current growth rate (partial containment). Africa and the Americas had lower CFRs but are growing fastest — suggesting the worst was yet to come for these regions at data cutoff.
7 — Speed of Spread

Days to 1,000 Cases: Early Containment Mattered

From full_grouped.csv, we can measure how many days elapsed between each country's first confirmed case and reaching 1,000 cumulative cases — a proxy for early epidemic velocity. Faster spread indicates either earlier seeding, higher transmissibility, or failure of containment.

Days from first case to 1,000 confirmed cases — selected countries (full_grouped.csv)
China reached 1,000 cases in just 3 days — reflecting the explosive initial outbreak in Wuhan. Turkey (11 days), Iran (12 days), and Switzerland/Norway (17 days) followed with rapid early spread. Georgia (140 days) and Uruguay (124 days) managed the longest delays, reflecting effective early border controls.
8 — Conclusions

What the Data Shows

Finding 1 — Testing confounds everything
Test capacity is the dominant source of cross-country noise (r = 0.621, p < 0.001). Yemen's 28.6% CFR and Africa's apparently low death rates are testing artefacts, not clinical realities. Comparisons of raw case counts across countries are largely meaningless without normalising for testing rates.
Finding 2 — Asia / Western Pacific contained Wave 1
Europe's median mortality (100/M) was 5.3× higher than Asia's (19/M) — a statistically significant difference (p < 0.001). Countries with SARS/MERS experience (South Korea, Singapore, Taiwan) activated testing, tracing, and masking protocols far earlier, suppressing the first wave before hospitals overloaded.
Finding 3 — Americas were the new epicentre by July 2020
At data cutoff, the Americas held 53% of global confirmed cases. The region's median weekly growth rate (13%/week) was 3× Europe's (4.3%), and the difference is statistically significant (p = 0.001). Colombia (+26%/week) and Argentina (+28%/week) were in rapid acceleration.
Finding 4 — Speed of containment was decisive
Countries reaching 1,000 cases in under 3 weeks (China, Turkey, Iran, Switzerland) lost the containment window immediately. Those delaying this threshold by 100+ days (Georgia, Uruguay, Namibia) bought time that allowed healthcare systems to prepare — translating into substantially lower per-capita deaths.
