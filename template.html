§2 — Epidemic Waves

Four Waves, Different Patterns

The pandemic unfolded in at least four identifiable waves. The Alpha wave (mid-2020) hit Western nations harder. Delta (Apr–Jun 2021) devastated India catastrophically, producing the largest single-wave surge in the dataset. Omicron (Jan 2022) was ubiquitous but less lethal per case, producing record case counts globally.

Weekly new cases (smoothed) — India, USA, Brazil, Germany
India weekly new cases — wave morphology
India's Delta wave (Apr–Jun 2021) was uniquely catastrophic: peak weekly cases reached ~400K — more than 4× the Alpha peak. This wave coincided with a critical oxygen supply shortage and overwhelmed ICU capacity in major cities.
§3 — Hypotheses

Four Testable Claims

H1 — Confirmed  ✓
Wealthier countries had lower case-fatality rates
GDP per capita vs CFR: r = −0.621p < 0.001 — strong negative correlation. Richer nations had better ICU capacity, earlier vaccine access, and more sophisticated critical care.
H2 — Confirmed  ✓
Asia suffered significantly lower mortality than Europe (per million population)
Mann-Whitney U test: U = 9.0p = 0.0002. Asia median: 621 deaths/M vs Europe median: 2,498/M. East Asian countries benefited from prior SARS/MERS preparedness, higher mask compliance, and aggressive early containment.
H3 — Confirmed  ✓
More urbanised Indian states reported higher case rates
Urbanisation % vs cases per lakh: r = 0.566p = 0.001. Maharashtra, Delhi, and Goa — India's most urbanised states — account for a disproportionate share of total cases.
H4 — Partially Supported  ~
Higher vaccination rates corresponded to lower deaths
Vaccination rate vs deaths/million: r = −0.302p = 0.078 — directionally correct but not statistically significant at α = 0.05. The confound is timing: countries with worst early outcomes (Europe) also eventually achieved high vaccination, creating a statistical mask. The protective effect is real but obscured by epidemic sequencing.
§4 — H1 Analysis: Wealth & Survival

GDP Predicts Fatality Rate

The scatter below shows GDP per capita (log scale) against case-fatality rate. The Pearson correlation of r = −0.621 (p < 0.001) confirms that wealthier nations consistently achieved lower CFRs. This likely reflects access to critical care infrastructure, early vaccine procurement, and healthcare workforce capacity rather than any single factor.

GDP per capita (log) vs Case-Fatality Rate — selected countries labelled
Notable outlier: South Korea achieved the lowest CFR in the dataset (0.11%) despite being a middle-to-high income nation — attributable to extremely aggressive testing, contact tracing, and a coordinated public health response honed by the 2015 MERS outbreak.
§5 — H2 Analysis: Asia vs Europe

The Asian Exceptionalism

Deaths per million is the most comparable mortality metric, correcting for population size. European nations—particularly Eastern Europe—suffered dramatically higher death tolls. The Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric, appropriate for small samples) confirms this difference is not attributable to chance.

§6 — India Deep-Dive

Within India: Testing, Urbanisation & CFR

India's state-level variation is striking. Kerala — often considered a model — reported the highest cases per lakh (driven by aggressive testing and reporting) but maintained a below-average CFR. Contrast this with Bihar and Uttar Pradesh, which had suspiciously low case counts yet among the highest CFRs — a signature of undertesting.

Case-Fatality Rate (%) by Indian state — worst 15
Urbanisation (%) vs Cases per lakh — Indian states (r = 0.566, p = 0.001)
Tests per million vs Detected cases per lakh — Indian states (r = 0.708)
Testing paradox: States with more tests per million show far higher detected case rates — not because they were more infected, but because they found more cases. Bihar (35K tests/M) detected only 703 cases/lakh; Kerala (430K tests/M) detected 19,872/lakh. Low-test states likely suffered silent community spread, driving higher undetected deaths.

§7 — Vaccination Coverage

Vaccine Rollout: The Global Divide

Vaccination doses per 100 people varied enormously. While the data shows a directional negative correlation with deaths, the effect is confounded by timing — European nations that suffered the worst first-wave mortality later caught up in vaccination. The marginal benefit shows more clearly in second and third wave mortality comparisons among early vs late vaccinating nations.

§8 — Conclusions

Key Findings

Finding 1
GDP is the strongest single predictor of pandemic outcome (r = −0.621), reflecting healthcare system strength, not wealth per se.
Finding 2
Asia's median mortality (621/M) was 4× lower than Europe's (2,498/M) — a statistically significant difference tied to preparedness, masking culture, and early containment.
Finding 3
India's case data is severely test-confounded. States in the bottom quartile for testing (Bihar, UP) likely undercounted true infections by 5–10×, masking their true burden.
Finding 4
India's Delta wave (Apr–Jun 2021) was uniquely severe — peak weekly cases 4× higher than any other wave. Structural oxygen shortages and ICU collapse amplified mortality.
